The morning I noticed something had to change, I was making coffee at six-thirty and I caught myself thinking, with no particular sadness about it, that I was probably going to be tired for the rest of my life.

I had told myself this for years, in slightly different words. Tired because the kids didn't sleep. Tired because of work. Tired because I was a bit older. Tired because — and this is the one that should have been the giveaway — I was tired. The reasoning was circular and I never noticed.

What I had done, without realising it, was build my entire identity around being a tired person. The way I planned my week. The energy I conserved for what I considered important. The things I quietly stopped doing because I didn't have the bandwidth. None of it felt like a problem. It felt like the shape of being fifty-one.

## The thing about tiredness that took me a long time to see

Tiredness is allowed to be a story you tell about yourself. There is no test for it. No one is going to challenge it. Your friends will not call you out on it because most of them are also a bit tired. Your doctor will not call you out on it because tiredness is one of the most common things adults present with and there is rarely a single neat answer.

So it sits there, undisturbed, until one day you stop and look at it properly and realise it is not actually a personality trait. It is a symptom. Of what, varies.

## What I changed first

I did not change anything dramatic. I went and had a comprehensive blood panel — the sort that looks at more than the standard six things — and I read the results carefully with someone who could explain them.

What I found was not surprising in retrospect but had been completely invisible to me. My inflammation markers were higher than they should have been. My hormonal picture was on the soft end of normal. My iron stores were depleted in a way that was not flagged on the basic panel I had had two years earlier. My fasting insulin — never tested previously — was elevated.

None of these were emergencies. All of them, together, were enough to explain why I was tired all the time.

## What I did next

I did the simple things first. Sleep. Strength training, properly, not the half-hearted version I had been doing. Cut the regular evening drink down to nothing on most nights. Started addressing the metabolic markers that had been creeping in the wrong direction.

I noticed something within about three weeks. Not a transformation. A shift. The afternoon slump was less brutal. I woke up actually feeling rested rather than functioning. The cognitive fog I had been calling "just being tired" lifted measurably.

Six months in, the difference was unmistakable, both subjectively and on the next panel.

## What I want anyone reading this to take away

Tiredness is a signal. It is not a character. If you have been tired for years, the question is not how to push through it more effectively. The question is what is producing it, and whether anything has actually looked.

There is a chance the answer is something like mine — a handful of unexceptional things, each tolerable on its own, that combined to produce a daily experience I had quietly written into my identity. There is a chance the answer is something else. The point is that the answer exists, and is findable, and most of the time it does not require dramatic intervention to start changing.

I am not the person I was at six-thirty in the morning two years ago, telling myself this was just how the rest of life was going to feel. I would not go back to that quietly resigned version of myself if I were paid to.

The first thing I would have changed, if I had known to, is the assumption that there was nothing to investigate. There almost always is.
